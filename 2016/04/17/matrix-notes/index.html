Notes of linear algebra

Just in case I might forget the very basic linear algebra.

Eigenvalues and eigenvectors**

For a square matrix $\boldsymbol{A}$, if we have $\boldsymbol{Ax} = \lambda\boldsymbol{x}$, then $\lambda$ is the eigenvalues and $\boldsymbol{x}$ is the eigenvector.

Spectral decomposition

Also called eigendecomposition, is the factorization of a matrix into a canonical form, whereby the matrix is represented in terms of its eigenvalues and eigenvectors. Only diagonalizable matrices can be factorized in this way. Symmetric matrices must be diagonalizable matrices.
$$ \boldsymbol{A} = \sum_{i=1}^n{\lambda_i\boldsymbol{e}_i\boldsymbol{e}_i^T} $$
or
$$ \boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{P\Lambda P}^T $$
where $\boldsymbol{\Lambda}$ is a diagonal matrix consisting of all eigenvalues of $\boldsymbol{A}$ and $\boldsymbol{P}$ is an $n\times n$ matrix consisting of the $n$ eigenvectors as columns.

Positive definite matrix

For a $k \times k$ symmetric matrix $\boldsymbol{A}$, if for any vector $\boldsymbol{x} \neq 0$ we have,
$$ \boldsymbol{x}^T\boldsymbol{Ax} > 0, $$
then we say $\boldsymbol{A}$ is positive-definite.

If $\boldsymbol{A}$ is positive definite, then $\boldsymbol{A}^{-1}$ is also positive definite, and the eigenvalues are $\frac{1}{\lambda_i}$, where $\lambda_i$ is the eigenvalue of $\boldsymbol{A}$.
$$ \boldsymbol{A} = \sum_{i=1}^n{\frac{1}{\lambda_i}\boldsymbol{e}_i\boldsymbol{e}_i^T} $$

Also, we can define the square root matrix of $boldsymbol{A}$, which is also positive definite and can be written as
$$ \boldsymbol{A} = \sum_{i=1}^n{\sqrt{\lambda_i}\boldsymbol{e}_i\boldsymbol{e}_i^T}. $$

$$ \boldsymbol{A}^{1/2}\boldsymbol{A}^{1/2} = \boldsymbol{A} $$

Cholesky decomposition

For a symmetric matrix $\boldsymbol{A}$, there exists a lower triangular matrix $\boldsymbol{L}$ with diagonal elements being 1 and a diagonal matrix $\boldsymbol{G}$ such that $\boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{LGL}^T$.

If $\boldsymbol{A}$ is positive definite, then the diagonal elements of $\boldsymbol{G}$ are positive. In this case, we have
$$ \boldsymbol{A} = \boldsymbol{L}\sqrt{\boldsymbol{G}}\sqrt{\boldsymbol{G}}\boldsymbol{L}^T = (\boldsymbol{L}\sqrt{\boldsymbol{G}})(\boldsymbol{L}\sqrt{\boldsymbol{G}})^T $$
where $\boldsymbol{L}\sqrt{\boldsymbol{G}}$ is again a lower triangular matrix and the square root is taken element by element.
